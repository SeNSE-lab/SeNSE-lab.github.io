The Hartmann Laboratory for Sensory and Neural Systems Engineering (SeNSE)

How do we sense and perceive the world around us?

Sensing is an active process. For example, the way you move your eyes influences what you see, which in turn influences the way you move your eyes. We study the sense of touch because it is closely intertwined with movement, and thus an ideal model for the study of active sensing. This type of research could ultimately help people disabled by stroke or brain injury.

Our work is highly interdisciplinary, combining biomechanical modeling, behavioral studies, neurophysiological recordings, computer simulations, and robotics.

Our laboratory has three broad aims:

  1. To perform basic research that answers fundamental scientific questions: Our research program focuses on “closing the loop” between sensing and movement. We use the rodent whisker (“vibrissal”) system as a model to study the sense of touch. We aim to understand all elements of the sensorimotor loop, from sensors to muscles. Some of our recent research results have quantified the mechanics of whisker bending and vibration, helped reveal the encoding properties of touch-sensitive neurons, uncovered how an animal might determine object shape based only on whisker-based touch, and developed a model for the muscles that control the whiskers.

  2. To construct toolsets for the research community and clinicians: Much of our research helps develop open-source toolsets to simulate animal sensing and motor behaviors across multiple species. Our work also aids clinicians who study facial motor nerve injury and recovery.

  3. To develop novel tools for engineering applications: We design and build proof-of-principle hardware systems (robots) that exploit the novel sensing capabilities of whiskers. Whiskers can operate in conditions when it is difficult to use cameras: in darkness, fog, glare, and when it is important to avoid emitting a signal. Sensors at the base of a whisker can be protected from the environment. Whiskers can also be used to sense texture and fluid currents. Potential applications include drones, pipelines, sea exploration, and geology.

FAQ

  • We use touch to move: Think about how difficult it is to move when your arm or leg has fallen asleep, or how difficult it is to speak after a numbing shot at the dentist's office. The sense of touch is closely linked to motor control and movement. Feedback from touch receptors informs the brain about the position and motion of body parts. This feedback is essential for fine motor skills, grasping and manipulating objects, balance and coordination. Without the sense of touch, every motor task becomes more difficult -- from walking, to buttoning clothes, to using tools.

  • We use touch to identify objects: The sense of touch helps us identify objects by feeling their shape, size, and texture. For example, it is easy for you to reach into your pocket or purse and — without looking — identify your cellphone, keys, or a coin. Somehow, your brain combines information about your hand movements and the touch-contacts that you make to enable you to detect, locate, and identify a particular object.

  • We use touch to sense where our limbs are: The sense of touch provides feedback about the position and movement of body parts. Without this feedback, you would experience a diminished sense of your body's position. The sense of the position and orientation of your limbs is called “proprioception.”

  • Understanding touch will help us build robots and assistive devices that can sense the world through touch: Understanding touch perception is crucial for developing effective touch-based technologies. This includes applications in virtual reality, teleoperation, robotics, and assistive devices. Some of our laboratory's research has involved constructing robots that sense the world by touching things.

The human hand is difficult to study. It has lots of muscles and it can move in many different ways. In engineering terms, the hand has many “degrees of freedom.” In addition, the mechanics of the skin is complicated, because it is “viscoelastic”-- it exhibits both viscous (liquid-like) and elastic (solid-like) behavior when it is deformed. Finally, it's difficult to precisely measure electrical signals in the human brain. For these and other reasons, we use rats and mice as models to study the sense of touch.

However… mice and rats typically use their whiskers more than their paws to tactually explore objects. If you used a slow-motion video camera to watch a rat, as our laboratory does, you would see that it continuously brushes its whiskers against objects between 5 and 25 times a second. This behavior is called "whisking." The rat touches different objects to figure out their location, size, shape, and texture. You can see some videos of rat whisking behavior here.

Whiskers are simpler to study than the hand, because they have no sensors along their length. They are like the hairs on your head-- it doesn't hurt when you get a haircut, and it doesn't hurt when we cut off the whiskers of a rat (don't worry-- we let them grow back). Because all sensing occurs at the whisker base, whiskers are mechanically “simple” compared to the human hand. Whiskers can be modeled as cantilever beams. Mechanical engineers have studied cantilever beams since the year 16xx, and probably longer.

The human brain contains a “somatopic map” of the human body. For example, neurons that respond to touching the thumb are adjacent to neurons that respond to touching the index finger. Neurons that respond to touching the index finger are between neurons that respond to touching the thumb and neurons that respond to touching the third finger… and so on.

In the same way that humans have five fingers on each hand (and there are neural maps of the hand in the human brain), rats have ~30 whiskers regularly arranged on each side of the face, and there are maps of the whiskers in the ratss brain. Scientists can record the electrical activity of neurons that respond to the touch of particular whiskers, or groups of whiskers, and determine how touch information is transformed at each stage, or how touch information is combined with movement.

About 50-80 laboratories worldwide study the rodent whisker system. The whisker system is so important in the field of neuroscience that researchers even have their own conference, held annually just before the Society for Neuroscience annual meeting.

Some laboratories use the whisker system to study brain plasticity or brain development and aging. Brain plasticity describes how the brain can reorganize its structure, functions, and connections in response to experiences, learning, and changes in the environment. Brain development describes how the brain changes from infancy through adulthood. For example, if you cut off all an animal's whiskers near birth, the neural circuits that represent the whiskers will not develop normally. If you cut off only one whisker, the regions of the brain that would have represented that whisker get taken over by neurons representing adjacent whiskers.

You use vision for a wide range of behaviors -- to walk, to scan the environment, to read, and to read the facial expressions of other people. Animals use their whiskers in an equally wide variety of behaviors, including locomotion, climbing, hunting, exploring, and social interactions… the list goes on.

Our laboratory has shown that:

  • Rats can move their whiskers asymmetrically-- sometimes even holding the whiskers completely still on one side of the face, while continuously moving whiskers on the other side.

  • Rats use their whiskers to sense which way the wind is blowing. Importantly, our work showed that rats not only can use their whiskers to send the wind direction, but they choose to use their whiskers to sense wind direction, even when other cues are available.
